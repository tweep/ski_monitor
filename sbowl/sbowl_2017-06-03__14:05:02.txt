<div class="h3" style="">Corn Snow</div>


<div style="padding-right:45px;">
<br>
In the early morning hours, off-piste terrain such as Nancy’s, Sugar Bowl, and the gated areas below Mt. Lincoln will be closed to start the day, giving the snow surface time to warm up.  As temps rise, Patrol will assess the conditions, then drop the lines so you can catch that perfect window of 'corn snow'.  In order to prevent deep ruts, Patrol will be closing those sections, later in the day, to allow the sun to “bake” the snow and reset for it next round of velvety corn snow. 
<br><br>
<b>What is corn snow?</b><br>
This elusive snow is actually very desirable and often falls in the “hero snow” category, but it can be hard to time. It is generally found in the spring when freeze/melt cycles affect the snow. Large grains of snow freeze together overnight, then they loosen as the sun warms them during the day. Corn refers to the snow during the time window when it has become soft and forgiving, but not too wet and slushy. In other words, it’s the “Goldilocks” of snow—not too hard, not too soft, but just right.
<br><br>
Spring conditions are ever changing and deserve awareness.  Ski or ride too early and you may find yourself on an undesirable and icy slope.  Come too late, and you could find the snow to be overly slushy. The key to a good corn harvest is all about timing.  True corn snow is caused by snow that has gone through repeated freeze-thaw cycles.  As the snow warms up, the bonds between the snow grains begin to melt, leading to a wonderful snow surface.  For a short period, true corn can be just as good as our old friend powder.  <a href="http://www.fsavalanche.org/corn-snow/
" target="_blank">Click here</a> to learn more about the science behind corn snow and spring conditions.
